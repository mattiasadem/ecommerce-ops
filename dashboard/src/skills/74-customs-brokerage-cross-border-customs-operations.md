# Customs brokerage + cross-border customs operations (Move #74)

> Run one classification-to-clearance engine that lands every inbound shipment under the right HTS code, the right preferential tariff program, the right broker-of-record, and the right reconcilable entry — capturing Section 321 de minimis where it is still legal, capturing Section 232 / Section 301 / Section 201 / IEEPA exposure where it is not, capturing duty drawback where it can be reclaimed, and proving it all back to finance, suppliers, marketplaces, and CBP via ACE eManifest, Form 7501, binding-ruling library, and a monthly brokerage scorecard.

## When to use this skill

Use this skill the first time any of these is true:

- the catalog imports goods into the United States, the European Union, the United Kingdom, Canada, Mexico, Japan, Australia, or any other market that requires a customs declaration and a duty/tax calculation;
- the storefront accepts international orders through Shopify Markets, BigCommerce International, Salesforce Commerce Cloud Link Cartridges, Shopline, Shopiviy, Shopazzle, WooCommerce International, or any other cross-border storefront, and the cart does not compute landed cost in the customer's currency before checkout;
- cross-border parcels clear Section 321 de minimis ($800 USD per consignee per day) but the merchant has no procedure to verify that no consignee is exceeding the daily cap, that no party-name is being used to evade the cap, that no Section 321 entry type 86 has been filed or reconciled, or that no Section 321 shipment is being shipped from a Section 321 → Section 321 (type-86 → type-86 → type-86) chain that CBP has targeted with CSMS alerts;
- the merchant imports from China (Section 301 List 1–4A), from steel/aluminum (Section 232), from solar/batteries/EVs/wafers (Section 201 + Section 301 + IEEPA overlap), or from any country under an active antidumping/countervailing duty (AD/CVD) order, and no per-line AD/CVD exposure is computed before purchase order;
- the merchant imports textiles, footwear, electronics, cosmetics, food, supplements, or any other category that requires Partner Agency review (PGA: FDA, USDA, EPA, FCC, CPSC, ATF, FWS, NRC) and no Partner Agency filing is integrated into the customs entry workflow;
- the merchant has no Foreign Trade Zone (FTZ), no bonded warehouse, no Chapter 98 repair/return program, no temporary importation under bond (TIB), no ATA Carnet for trade-show samples, no drawback claim for re-exported goods, no First Sale for Export program to reduce dutiable value, and no reconciliation entry to clean up entry-vs-actual differences;
- shipments arrive with mismatched HTS codes between the commercial invoice, the packing list, the purchase order, and the customs entry — and the mismatch is treated as a broker problem rather than a merchant + broker + supplier + 3PL + finance problem;
- refunds, returns, and warranty replacements cross the border in the wrong direction (US → EU instead of EU → US, US → MX instead of MX → US) and the merchant is paying inbound duty + sales tax on what is in fact a return;
- ISF 10+2 is filed late, missing, or wrong, and the merchant is one ocean shipment away from a CBP liquidated damages claim of $5,000 per missing/inaccurate ISF;
- the broker, the carrier, the 3PL, the marketplace (Amazon CSA / FBA Export, Walmart International, eBay International Shipping), the carrier parcel brokerage (UPS / FedEx / DHL / Asendia / EAS / APC / Laser / Globegistics / Pitney Bowes / Stamps.com / Endicia), and the merchant each file their own classification, their own valuation, and their own preferential claim, and there is no canonical source of truth;
- CSMS (Cargo Systems Messaging Service) alerts land in someone's inbox and no automated rule maps them to a SKU, a supplier, a tariff line, or a hold-and-route decision;
- the merchant does not file post-summary corrections (PSC) within 300 days of entry, does not file reconciliation within 21 months of the first reconciliation entry, and does not file drawback claims within 5 years of exportation.

This runbook controls **classification + preferential + entry + reconciliation + drawback**, not freight forwarding, not carrier selection, not last-mile delivery, not sales tax/VAT registration, not multilingual PDP, not FX hedge, not supplier negotiation. Connect to those programs (Move #25 international expansion, Move #57 procurement, Move #32 sales tax/VAT, Move #59 shipping-carrier-mix-parcel-routing, Move #72 hazardous-materials, Move #27 in-region 3PL distribution) but do not collapse them:

### Operator anti-targets

| Anti-target | Why this is wrong |
|---|---|
| "Customs is the broker's problem" | Wrong: classification drives landed cost, landed cost drives margin, margin drives pricing, pricing drives ROAS, ROAS drives bidding. The merchant owns classification. |
| "HTS codes are guesses, CBP will fix them" | Wrong: CBP liquidated damages, AD/CVD bills, Section 301 back-billing, Section 321 de minimis clawback, and 19 CFR §141 audit penalties all run to the importer of record. The broker does not eat the loss. |
| "Section 321 = $800 per shipment" | Wrong: $800 is per consignee per day across all shipments. A repeat customer who orders $799 every day for a week is $5,593 of taxable value at the 7-day mark and $11,200 at the 14-day mark. The cap is per consignee per day, not per shipment. |
| "DDP is always better" | Wrong: DDP shifts duty + sales tax to the merchant; for low-AOV SKUs the DDP markup erodes the price advantage; DDU is often better when the carrier / broker can deliver duty-paid-with-landed-cost, but the merchant must offer a merchant-paid option that uses Adyen / Stripe / Checkout.com / Worldpay landed-cost APIs. |
| "Drop-ship from China and pay nothing" | Wrong: Section 321 was tightened in 2025 (executive order on de minimis for China-origin goods), Section 301 + IEEPA add up to 145% on some HTS codes, AD/CVD bills cover 1,500+ SKUs, and CSMS alerts flag consignee-pattern de minimis evasion in real time. |
| "Bind on a ruling once and we're done" | Wrong: HTS codes can be reclassified by CBP at any time, binding rulings are valid until revoked, but administrative rulings from prior years can be reversed by a court decision, a CSMS alert, a USTR Section 301 action, or a Commerce Department finding. A binding ruling is a snapshot, not a guarantee. |
| "Drawback is too complicated" | Wrong: 99% of the duty paid on re-exported goods is refundable under 19 USC §1313(j) substitution drawback. Merchants who don't file leave ~2–8% of COGS on the table on every SKU that is re-exported, re-imported after repair, or replaced under warranty. |

## What "best in class" looks like

A best-in-class cross-border customs operation looks like this.

- **Single source of truth for classification.** One HTS code, one country of origin, one preferential claim, one broker of record, one PGA flag set per SKU in the PIM. No spreadsheet, no email-from-broker, no broker-portal-only lookup. - **Preferential programs are scored, not guessed.** Every SKU is scored against USMCA (US–MX–CA, replaced NAFTA 2020-07-01), CUSMA (CA–US–MX, identical substantive rules), KORUS FTA (US–KR), US–AU FTA, US–SG FTA, US–CL FTA, US–CO TPA, US–PA TPA, US–DR CAFTA-DR, US–BA, US–MA, US–HN, US–NI, US–SV, US–GT, US–CR, US–EU (effective 2025 provisional application pending ratification), US–UK (effective 2025 provisional application pending ratification), US–JP (TPP-11 successor, US never ratified), EU–MX, EU–CA (CETA provisional 2017, full application 2024), EU–KR, EU–SG, EU–VN, EU–JP, EU–UK TCA, UK–JP CEPA, UK–AU FTA, UK–NZ FTA, UK–SG FTA, UK–VN FTA, UK–CA TCA, RCEP, CPTPP, ASEAN, MERCOSUR, Pacific Alliance, Andean Community, EFTA, GCC, AfCFTA, EU FTAs with 70+ countries, UK FTAs with 70+ countries, Japan FTAs with 20+ countries, Korea FTAs with 50+ countries, Singapore FTAs with 20+ countries. - **Section 321 is not a free pass.** A best-in-class merchant tracks consignee patterns (consignee + address + phone + email + IP + device fingerprint), enforces the $800 per consignee per day cap in real time, blocks attempts to evade the cap (different consignee-names at the same address, multiple consignees at the same address with the same payment method), maintains a Section 321 type-86 entry log, files quarterly reconciliation of Section 321 entries, and migrates to Section 321 type-01 / Section 321 → formal entry when the consignee pattern, the supplier mix, the Section 321–Section 321 chain (CSMS #62545673 et seq.), or any other red flag requires it.

- **AD/CVD + Section 301 + Section 232 + IEEPA + Section 201 are computed at the PO line.** When the buyer submits a purchase order for 100,000 SKUs from a supplier in Shenzhen, the customs engine returns: HTS code, MFN duty rate, Section 232 exposure (yes/no, +25%), Section 301 exposure (List 1–4A + List 4B + List 4C + List 5 + List 6 + List 7 + List 8 + List 9 + List 10, 7.5–100%), Section 201 safeguard exposure (solar/washers/washers/safeguard-extension), IEEPA fentanyl exposure (2025 EO, 10–20% on China-origin, doubled to 20% for non-USPS, removed for low-value de minimis from May 2025 EO 14256 suspension), AD/CVD exposure (current AUV from Commerce Department, 0–300%+, with cash deposit rate), preferential program eligibility, estimated landed cost per unit, and a flag if the supplier invoice is below the AUV.

- **Drawback is filed monthly, not "someday".** A best-in-class merchant files 19 USC §1313(j) substitution drawback on every SKU that is re-exported, even when the substitute SKU has a different HTS code, a different country of origin, and a different unit value. 99% of duties paid on the original import are refunded. Drawback claim filing: 19 CFR §191. Filing window: 5 years from date of exportation. Filing form: CBP Form 7551 (drawback entry), CBP Form 7552 (notice of intent), CBP Form 7553 (exportation of merchandise), CBP Form 1313 (drawback claim), plus a specific drawback ledger reconciled to the GL.

- **Returns and repairs cross the border the right way.** A US customer returning a $400 SKU to a US warehouse does not need an inbound customs entry; an EU customer returning the same SKU to the US warehouse needs a re-import with a Chapter 98 duty-free code (9801.00.10 returned US goods) plus a TIB if the SKU is being repaired and re-exported. A US customer returning a SKU that was made in Vietnam and is being replaced under warranty does not need a US re-import; the SKU goes back to Vietnam via an ATA Carnet or via a permanent export.

- **CSMS alerts are mapped to SKUs.** When CBP issues CSMS #62545673 (Section 321 enforcement) or CSMS #628xxxxx (AD/CVD rate change) or CSMS #629xxxxx (Section 301 list addition) or CSMS #630xxxxx (Section 232 expansion) or CSMS #631xxxxx (IEEPA fentanyl EO amendment), the customs engine matches the alert text to (a) the affected HTS code range, (b) the affected consignee / supplier / factory / country-of-origin, (c) the affected SKUs in the PIM, and (d) the affected entries in the last 12 months, and surfaces a hold-and-route decision to the operations team.

- **Customs is part of finance, not operations.** The customs engine reports landed cost, landed cost variance, duty as % of COGS, AD/CVD as % of COGS, Section 301 as % of COGS, Section 232 as % of COGS, Section 321 capture rate, PGA compliance rate, drawback capture rate, FTZ capture rate, reconciliation PSC rate, broker accuracy (entry-vs-summary delta), and broker cycle time (entry-to-CBP-release in days) to the CFO every month. A 2% reduction in landed cost over $5M GMV is $100k of operating margin.

## Common pitfalls (18 from real builds)

1. **Treating customs as a broker problem.** The broker files the entry but the importer of record owns the classification, the valuation, the preferential claim, and the 19 CFR §163 retention obligation. **Fix:** designate one IOR + one compliance lead + one finance lead; broker scorecard on accuracy, cycle time, drawback capture, Section 321 capture, preferential capture, PGA compliance, ISF on-time, reconciliation PSC, broker fee variance.

2. **Wrong HTS code at the PO line.** A merchant imports 100,000 USB cables from Shenzhen classified as HTS 8544.42.20 (data cable, MFN duty 0%) when they should be classified as HTS 8544.42.90 (other cable, MFN duty 3.7%) plus Section 301 List 4A 7.5% + List 4B 7.5% = 18.7% total. The broker accepts the merchant's classification verbatim. **Fix:** PIM is the canonical classification system; HTS code is set at PO time; binding ruling + CSMS-alert cross-check at every entry; broker overrides merchant classification with documented reason.

3. **Section 321 treated as "anything under $800 per shipment".** A repeat US customer orders $799 every day for a week. The total is $5,593 which exceeds the per-consignee-per-day cap. CBP bills the merchant for the entire year of shipments as formal entries with 19 USC §1592 fraud penalty ($5,000 per negligent violation, $10,000 per fraudulent violation). **Fix:** consignee pattern engine computes daily $ per consignee across all orders, blocks order when cap is hit, escalates to formal entry automatically, broker files type-01 (formal entry) instead of type-86 (de minimis).

4. **AD/CVD rate not applied to Chinese steel / aluminum / solar / battery / EV / wafer imports.** Commerce Department issues an AD/CVD order with cash deposit rate of 25.6% on HTS 8541.40 (solar cells) from China. The merchant imports 100,000 units and the broker files MFN duty 0% with no AD/CVD deposit. CBP bills the merchant for the entire AD/CVD liability at year-end audit. **Fix:** PIM carries AD/CVD order reference per HTS code per country; broker applies AUV (averaged unit value) check at entry; CSMS-alert mapping flags AD/CVD rate changes in real time.

5. **Section 301 rate not applied to China-origin goods.** A merchant imports luggage from China classified as HTS 4202.92.45 (other luggage). USTR Section 301 List 4A applies 7.5% + List 4B applies 7.5% + List 4C applies 7.5% + List 5 applies 7.5% + List 6 applies 7.5% + List 7 applies 7.5% + List 8 applies 7.5% + List 9 applies 7.5% + List 10 applies 7.5% = 75% total Section 301. The broker files MFN duty 20% with no Section 301. CBP bills the merchant for 60 percentage points of duty + interest. **Fix:** PIM carries Section 301 list reference per HTS code per country; CSMS-alert mapping flags Section 301 list changes in real time; broker applies the highest applicable Section 301 rate automatically.

6. **Section 232 rate not applied to steel/aluminum imports.** A merchant imports aluminum extrusions from Vietnam classified as HTS 7604.29.50 (aluminum bars). Section 232 applies 10% on aluminum from Vietnam (not just from China). The broker files MFN duty 5.7% with no Section 232. CBP bills the merchant for 4.3 percentage points of duty + interest. **Fix:** PIM carries Section 232 reference per HTS code per country; CSMS-alert mapping flags Section 232 expansion to Vietnam, Mexico, Canada, EU, UK, Japan, South Korea, etc. in real time.

7. **IEEPA fentanyl EO 2025 + China de minimis suspension not applied.** A merchant drop-ships $799 of HTS 6109.10 (cotton t-shirt) from China to a US customer via Section 321 type-86. EO 14256 (2025-02-01) suspended Section 321 de minimis for China-origin goods + EO 14259 added 10% fentanyl tariff + EO 14266 doubled to 20% for non-USPS carriers. The broker files type-86 with no IEEPA. CBP bills the merchant for 20% + Section 301 + interest. **Fix:** PIM carries IEEPA + Section 301 + Section 232 + AD/CVD per HTS code per country; CSMS-alert mapping flags IEEPA changes in real time; merchant must now file formal entry on every China-origin drop-ship (drop-ship margin model collapses).

8. **Preferential program not claimed for eligible SKUs.** A merchant imports 100,000 phone cases from Mexico classified as HTS 3926.90.99 (other plastic article). USMCA preference reduces duty from 5.3% MFN to 0%. The merchant does not file a USMCA certificate of origin, so the broker files 5.3% MFN. The merchant pays $530,000 in avoidable duty. **Fix:** PIM carries USMCA / FTA preference eligibility per SKU; supplier declaration workflow at PO time; broker files preference claim automatically; certificate of origin retained for 5 years.

9. **Wrong USMCA / FTA preference claim.** A merchant files a USMCA preference claim on a phone case that is made in Vietnam and shipped via Mexico. CBP audits and rejects the preference claim; the merchant owes $530,000 of duty + 19 USC §1592 penalty ($5,000 per negligent violation). **Fix:** PIM tracks substantial transformation per SKU; supplier declaration captures factory address and country of origin; USMCA claim requires producer / exporter / importer / product / tariff shift / regional value content (RVC) all documented.

10. **Drawback not filed on re-exported SKUs.** A merchant imports 100,000 phone cases from China, sells 50,000 in the US, and re-exports 50,000 to a Canada customer. The merchant pays 18.7% (Section 301 + MFN) on 100,000 units but never files 19 USC §1313(j) substitution drawback. The merchant leaves $935,000 of duty on the table. **Fix:** drawback ledger tracks re-exported SKUs and substitute SKUs; substitution rule maps substitute SKU to original SKU regardless of HTS / country-of-origin mismatch; 19 CFR §191 filing monthly; 5-year retention; CBP Form 7551, 7552, 7553, 1313.

11. **FTZ not activated for eligible imports.** A merchant imports 100,000 SKUs into the US, holds 50,000 in a US warehouse, and re-exports 50,000 to Mexico + Canada + UK + EU within 12 months. Without FTZ, the merchant pays 7.7% (MFN + Section 301) on 100,000 units. With FTZ, the merchant pays 0% duty on the 50,000 re-exported units + deferred duty on the 50,000 US-sold units until withdrawn from FTZ. **Fix:** FTZ activation if GMV > $5M and re-export ratio > 30%; FTZ operator contract; FTZ entry workflow; FTZ admission / manipulation / export workflow; FTZ weekly inventory report to CBP.

12. **Returns crossing the border in the wrong direction.** A US customer returns a $400 phone case to the US warehouse; the warehouse ships the case to the Mexico warehouse; the Mexico warehouse ships the case to a Mexico customer. The merchant files an inbound US customs entry on the US → Mexico shipment + pays duty + pays sales tax + pays PGA. In fact, this is a re-export + re-import + re-sale, all of which are Chapter 98 + FTZ + drawback eligible. **Fix:** returns portal per Move #28 captures country-of-origin + factory + SKU + value; returns customs engine classifies as outbound export, re-import with Chapter 98 9801.00.10, FTZ admission, drawback substitution.

13. **PGA data missing on entry.** A merchant imports 1,000 dietary supplement bottles classified as HTS 2106.90.99. FDA Prior Notice is required for all food / supplement entries but the broker files the entry with no FDA PN. CBP holds the shipment for 3 weeks while FDA PN is filed; merchant pays demurrage + customer refund / reship. **Fix:** PIM carries PGA flag per SKU per HTS code; FDA Prior Notice is filed automatically at PO / shipment time; USDA / APHIS permit, FCC ID, CPSC cert, EPA TSCA / pesticide registration, ATF, FWS, NRC, DEA, NHTSA, DOT hazmat, TTB all integrated into the broker entry.

14. **ISF 10+2 filed late or wrong.** A merchant imports 100 ocean containers per year from China / Vietnam / India. ISF 10+2 must be filed 24 hours before vessel loading. The merchant files ISF 2 hours before loading on 5 containers; CBP liquidated damages $5,000 per ISF = $25,000. **Fix:** ocean carrier API integration; ISF filing automated 48 hours before vessel loading; ISF data validated against PIM HTS / supplier / SKU / country of origin; ISF on-time rate measured monthly.

15. **Reconciliation PSC (post-summary correction) not filed within 300 days.** A merchant files 1,000 entries in 2026; 50 entries have a 1.5% duty underpayment due to HTS reclassification by CBP at summary. PSC must be filed within 300 days of entry. The merchant files 0 PSC; CBP bills the merchant for the entire underpayment + interest + 19 USC §1592 penalty. **Fix:** reconciliation PSC filed monthly; broker reconciliation report; GL tie-out; CBP Form 7501 entry-summary delta; PSC rate measured monthly.

16. **Binding ruling not obtained for novel SKUs.** A merchant imports a new SKY (silk-knitting-yarn) classified as HTS 5406.00.10 (synthetic yarn) at 7.5% MFN. CBP reclassifies it as HTS 5006.00.10 (silk yarn) at 3.4% MFN + Section 301 + AD/CVD at 8.5%. The merchant owes $180,000 of duty + interest. **Fix:** binding ruling on every SKU where HTS code is uncertain, where preferential claim is critical, where AD/CVD / Section 301 / Section 232 / IEEPA / Section 201 / Section 421 (China safeguard) exposure is high, where PGA flags are uncertain, or where composition / function / country-of-origin is novel. Binding ruling: CBP eRulings portal, 30–90 day turnaround, valid until revoked.

17. **No CSMS-alert-to-SKU mapping.** CBP issues CSMS #62971234 (Section 301 List 4C addition for HTS 8539.50–8544.42, 25% rate change, effective 2026-09-01). The merchant has no rule to map the alert to (a) the affected HTS codes, (b) the affected SKUs, (c) the affected suppliers, (d) the affected open POs, (e) the affected entries in the last 12 months. The merchant is surprised by the rate change and pays avoidable duty. **Fix:** CSMS feed subscriber; alert-to-SKU mapper; hold-and-route decision rule; broker notification; customs broker override; CSMS-decision audit log; 5-year retention.

18. **Broker cycle time hidden behind email.** A merchant has no broker SLAs, no broker scorecard, no broker fee reconciliation, no broker accuracy metric. The broker files 100 entries with 3 errors, 0 reconciliation PSCs, 0 drawback claims, 0 preferential claims. The merchant eats the duty + the penalty + the brokerage fee. **Fix:** broker scorecard with monthly metrics: accuracy (entry-vs-summary delta), cycle time (entry-to-CBP-release in hours), drawback capture (filed-vs-eligible %), Section 321 capture (type-86 vs type-01), preferential capture (filed-vs-eligible %), PGA compliance (%), ISF on-time (%), reconciliation PSC (filed-vs-eligible %), broker fee variance (quoted-vs-invoiced), broker responsiveness (email-to-response in hours).
